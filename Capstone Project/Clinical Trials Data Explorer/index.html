Clinical Trials is a place to learn about clinical studies from around the world.

Clinical trials are research studies that test how well new medical approaches work in people. Each study answers scientific questions and tries to find better ways to prevent, screen for, diagnose, or treat a disease. Clinical trials may also compare a new treatment to a treatment that is already available.

Every clinical trial has a protocol, or action plan, for conducting the trial. The plan describes what will be done in the study, how it will be conducted, and why each part of the study is necessary. Each study has its own rules about who can take part. Some studies need volunteers with a certain disease. Some need healthy people. Others want just men or just women.

Trial Details

Trials are crucial processes in medical research designed to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of new treatments, interventions, or medical devices.

These trials contribute significantly to advancements in healthcare, shaping the future of medicine.

Protocols:

Every clinical trial follows a detailed plan known as a protocol.

This document outlines the study's objectives, methodology, participant eligibility criteria, and the plan for data collection and analysis.

Trial Phases:

  • Phase I:
    • Objective: Assess safety.
    • Focus: Determine the safety profile of the intervention in a small group of participants.
    • Key: Investigate dosage, side effects, and how the body processes the treatment.
  • Phase II:
    • Objective: Explore efficacy.
    • Focus: Evaluate the effectiveness of the treatment in a larger group of participants.
    • Key: Gather preliminary data on whether the intervention works for the intended purpose.
  • Phase III:
    • Objective: Examine overall benefits and risks.
    • Focus: Assess the treatment's effectiveness, monitor side effects, and compare it to standard.
    • Key: Provide critical information for regulatory approval.
  • Phase IV:
    • Objective: Post-marketing surveillance.
    • Focus: Continued monitoring after the treatment is in the market.
    • Key: Identify rare or long-term side effects and ensure ongoing safety.
